## Question Generation Assessment

### The Futurist — Cognitive Mode: Trend Extrapolation

The Futurist operates through **documented trend projection**. Every question anchors itself in a specific real-world trend, then extrapolates its implications for the topic at hand. The signature structure is: "Given the documented/accelerating trend toward [X]..." followed by a forward-looking design question. Nearly every question contains explicit trend citations ("documented trend toward," "accelerating adoption of," "growing emphasis on," "research shows").

Emotional register: authoritative, evidence-anchored, consultative. The Futurist presents as a strategic advisor who has read the reports and is translating them into planning questions. There is very little imagination or surprise — the tone is sober, professional, and anticipatory.

**Volume and structure:**
- Space party: 17 questions / 5 clusters (listed as 5 in metadata, actually 4 headers with content + total line)
- Tool library: 18 questions / 5 clusters
- Habit tracker: 17 questions / 5 clusters (listed as 5 in metadata, actually 6 headers)

Highly consistent output (7.4 KB avg, CV=4.4%), always targeting 5 clusters, always producing 17-18 questions. This is the most structurally predictable persona in the evaluation set.

### The Visionary — Cognitive Mode: Paradigm Reimagination

(Summarized from I03.) The Visionary operates through **aspiration and reconstruction**. Questions start from a reimagined future state and work backward. The signature structure is: "What if we completely reimagined [concept]?" followed by vivid description and design implications. No trend citations — the Visionary invents futures rather than extrapolating them.

Emotional register: inspiring, expansive, possibility-oriented. The Visionary paints pictures of states that don't yet exist and invites exploration of how to get there.

**Visionary clusters:** Transformative Experience Design, Redefining "Space", Rethinking Participation, Reframing Logistics, Architecture of Wonder

**Overlap assessment:** Both touch entertainment, memory, and participation. But the Futurist frames these as trend-responsive positioning questions ("How might AR projection technologies transform at-home birthday parties as consumer projector prices continue declining?") while the Visionary frames them as paradigm questions ("What if we designed for one moment of genuine transcendence rather than two hours of themed engagement?"). The Futurist's clusters read like a consulting deck; the Visionary's read like a design manifesto.

#### Tool Library

The Futurist has "documented shift away from gamification"; the Visionary has "what if we designed for intrinsic motivation without any extrinsic rewards at all." These probe similar themes but the Futurist asks about positioning strategy while the Visionary asks about fundamental redesign.

## Temporal Lens Anti-Pattern Analysis

The "temporal lens" anti-pattern occurs when a question merely adds trend framing ("Given the documented trend toward X...") to an otherwise standard question that any persona could ask. The trend citation is decorative, not generative — it doesn't change the question's territory or the answer space.

### Space Party (experiential topic) — Anti-pattern rate: ~41% (7/17)

Questions exhibiting temporal lens decoration:

1. "How might the documented shift away from single-use plastics influence material choices for space-themed decorations?" — Standard sustainability question with trend citation added.
2. "How are parental expectations around waste and sustainability changing for children's events?" — Standard party planning question with trend framing.
3. "How are parental expectations around educational value in children's entertainment evolving?" — Standard parent consideration with trend language.
4. "How are changing socialization patterns for young children influencing birthday party structures?" — Standard social development question with trend framing.
5. "What role are smaller, more intimate celebrations playing in response to 'party inflation' trends?" — Standard party scale question with trend label.
6. "What role is user-generated content playing in children's events?" — Standard photo/memory question with trend framing.
7. "How are privacy and digital footprint concerns influencing documentation practices for children's events?" — Standard privacy question with trend language.

These questions would be essentially unchanged if you removed the trend citations. "How should we handle sustainability in party supplies?" is the same question without "documented shift away from single-use plastics."

### Tool Library (civic topic) — Anti-pattern rate: ~22% (4/18)

1. "How might the documented decline in homeownership among younger generations reshape tool inventory priorities?" — Trend-decorated inventory question.
2. "Given the accelerating trend toward solo and single-person households, what individual-focused adaptations?" — Demographic framing on standard user needs question.
3. "What succession model accounts for the documented 'founder fatigue' pattern?" — Standard volunteer management question with trend citation.
4. "What climate resilience adaptations are necessary given the accelerating frequency of extreme weather events?" — Standard preparedness question.

### Habit Tracker (technical topic) — Anti-pattern rate: ~24% (4/17)

1. "Given the documented shift toward AI copilots as the primary interface pattern, how should habit-tracking intelligence be positioned?" — This one is borderline; the trend genuinely shapes the question.
2. "How does the product strategy change given the documented trajectory of commoditized AI features?" — Trend-decorated differentiation question.
3. "How does the product strategy account for the documented trajectory of subscription fatigue?" — Standard monetization question with trend framing.
4. "Given the accelerating trend toward web standards as the cross-platform layer, what native-like capabilities are worth waiting for?" — Standard PWA vs native question.

### Anti-pattern summary

| Topic | Anti-pattern Rate | Notes |
|-------|------------------|-------|
| Space party (experiential) | 41% (7/17) | Highest rate. Trend language often feels forced on a personal/experiential topic. |
| Tool library (civic) | 22% (4/18) | Moderate. Trends are more naturally relevant to civic infrastructure. |
| Habit tracker (technical) | 24% (4/17) | Moderate. Tech trends are often genuinely relevant to product strategy. |
| **Overall** | **29% (15/52)** | Close to the predicted ~35% on experiential topics; lower on civic/technical. |

**Finding:** The anti-pattern is topic-sensitive. On the experiential space party topic, nearly half the Futurist's questions are standard planning questions with trend decoration bolted on. On civic and technical topics, the trend lens is more often genuinely generative — it changes the answer space, not just the framing.

## Cross-Topic Patterns

### Structural consistency
The Futurist is the most structurally consistent persona evaluated: always 17-18 questions, always 5 clusters, always trend-citation-heavy. The 4.4% CV in file size reflects near-identical output structure regardless of topic.

### Topic sensitivity
**The Futurist performs best on civic and technical topics** where trends are genuinely relevant to strategic planning. On the tool library, questions about ESG funding, IoT integration, platform cooperatives, and climate resilience funding open genuinely useful strategic territory. On the habit tracker, questions about AI copilots, category convergence, and data sovereignty address real product strategy challenges.

**The Futurist performs weakest on experiential topics** where trend language feels imposed. "How are attention spans evolving for 5-year-olds exposed to high-digital environments?" is a reasonable question, but wrapping it in trend language doesn't add value over what the Analyst or Strategist would produce.

### Consistency of cognitive mode
The Futurist never breaks character — every single question across all three topics uses trend extrapolation as its cognitive entry point. This is both a strength (reliable, predictable coverage) and a weakness (no surprise, no creative leaps). Unlike the Provocateur which produces occasional creative explosions (21 questions on tool library), the Futurist maintains steady output without peaks.

## Selflessness Assessment

**Which Phase 3 personas could productively answer the Futurist's questions?**

The Futurist's strategic/analytical questions are broadly answerable:
- **Strategist/Analyst:** Natural fit for positioning, competitive, and risk questions
- **Pragmatist:** Can address infrastructure, operations, and implementation timeline questions
- **Systems Thinker:** Can address interconnection, resilience, and ecosystem questions
- **Futurist itself:** Many questions are self-answerable (a weakness — "how should we position for trend X?" invites the same trend-extrapolation thinking that generated the question)
- **Visionary:** Can reframe strategic questions into paradigm-level responses
- **Community Builder/Advocate:** Can address demographic and community shift questions

**Selflessness rating: MODERATE.** The Futurist generates questions that many personas can answer, but a significant portion (estimated 25-30%) are most naturally answered by the Futurist itself or a similar strategic persona. The "how should we position for [trend]?" format often invites trend-based answers rather than diverse perspectives. Compare this to the Provocateur (high selflessness — generates questions it cannot answer) or the Visionary (high selflessness — envisions without specifying implementation).

## Convergence Estimate: Futurist vs Visionary

**Thematic convergence: ~30-35%** — Both personas address similar macro-themes (post-app futures, sustainability, community evolution, privacy) on roughly a third of their questions.

**Question-level convergence: ~15-20%** — When you compare actual question territory (what answer space is opened), only about 1 in 5-6 questions from the Futurist would be meaningfully redundant if the Visionary were also present. The treatment difference (strategic positioning vs. paradigm reimagination) means even thematically overlapping questions open different answer spaces.

**Phase 3 convergence: ~19-20%** (consistent with provided quantitative context) — At the brainstorming stage, Futurist and Visionary questions would produce substantially different idea territory.

## Recommendation

### Phase 2B Roster Recommendation: **Conditional — Include only if roster expands to 6+ AND lacks strategic coverage**

**Rationale for conditional rather than strong include:**

1. **The Futurist is a strategic persona, not a creative one.** Despite its "forward-looking" branding, its dimension profile (56% Strategic, 21% Analytical, 0% Creative) places it squarely in the analytical camp. It does not fill the same role as the Visionary and should not be evaluated as a Visionary alternative.

2. **Moderate anti-pattern rate.** At 29% overall (41% on experiential topics), a significant fraction of the Futurist's output is trend-decorated standard questions. This is below the ~35% threshold predicted for experiential topics but above what would indicate strong generative value.

3. **Limited selflessness.** Many Futurist questions invite trend-based answers, narrowing the range of Phase 3 personas that can productively engage. The Visionary and Provocateur score higher on selflessness — their questions invite diverse perspectives by design.

4. **Genuine unique territory exists.** The Futurist's competitive positioning, risk anticipation, and infrastructure planning questions are genuinely useful and not produced by other personas. Questions about ESG funding, IoT risk, platform cooperative governance, and AI-agent API design open strategic territory that the Visionary, Provocateur, and most analytical personas miss.

5. **Strong topic sensitivity.** The Futurist adds most value on civic and technical topics where external trends genuinely shape the project. On experiential topics, its value drops significantly. This makes it a good conditional pick for topic types where strategic positioning matters.

**Specific roster guidance:**

- **For medium effort (3 personas):** Do NOT include. The Futurist's strategic territory is partially covered by the Strategist/Analyst, and its limited selflessness makes it a weaker Phase 2B contributor than the Visionary or Provocateur.
- **For high effort (5 personas):** Do NOT include as one of the 5. Visionary + Provocateur cover generative territory; 3 analytical personas cover strategic/analytical territory more broadly.
- **For expanded high effort (6-7 personas):** INCLUDE if the topic is civic or technical. The Futurist's unique strategic questions about competitive landscape, funding mechanisms, and infrastructure planning add distinct value at this roster size. EXCLUDE if the topic is experiential/personal.

## Notes

1. **The Futurist and Visionary are NOT a natural pair** despite both being "forward-looking." Their cognitive modes are fundamentally different: trend extrapolation (analytical) vs. paradigm reimagination (creative). The Futurist is better compared to the Strategist or Analyst than to the Visionary. The "forward-looking pair" framing is misleading — what matters is cognitive mode, not temporal orientation.

2. **The "documented trend" citation pattern** is the Futurist's most distinctive structural feature and also its most problematic. It creates an impression of authority but frequently decorates standard questions with trend language rather than generating genuinely trend-dependent insights. A revised persona prompt could address this by requiring that the cited trend must change the answer space — if removing the trend citation doesn't change the question, the question should be reworked.

3. **The Futurist's consistency is a double-edged sword.** The 4.4% CV and identical cluster counts suggest a highly templated output pattern. This makes it reliable but also means it generates no surprises. Contrast with the Provocateur (variable output, occasional creative explosions) or the Visionary (consistent but each question is genuinely novel in framing).

4. **Connection to I03:** The I03 investigation recommended including both Visionary and Provocateur for generative coverage. This I14 investigation confirms that the Futurist would NOT serve as a substitute for either — it occupies different dimension territory entirely. If the roster needs a strategic/trend voice, the Futurist fills that role; but it does not overlap with or replace the Visionary's creative paradigm-breaking function.

5. **Strongest individual question from the Futurist** (across all topics): "Given the documented trend toward platform cooperatives and community-owned infrastructure, what governance structures future-proof against extraction?" (tool library). This question opens genuinely useful strategic territory about ownership models that other personas don't address. It demonstrates the Futurist at its best — when the cited trend is specific, relevant, and genuinely shapes the question's answer space.

6. **Weakest individual question from the Futurist** (across all topics): "How are parental expectations around waste and sustainability changing for children's events, and what implications does this have for party favor design?" (space party). This is a standard party planning question with trend language layered on. Any persona could ask "what about sustainable party favors?" — the trend framing adds nothing.
