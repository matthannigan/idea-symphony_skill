## Evidence

### Orientation: Structural Equity vs Experiential Narrative

The two personas approach human-centeredness from opposite directions. The Audience Advocate asks *"Who is excluded and how do we include them?"* while the Storyteller asks *"What would this feel like to experience?"*

**Audience Advocate signature phrases** (consistent across all 3 topics):
- "From the community's perspective..."
- "The people we serve might experience this as..."
- "This could inadvertently create barriers for..."
- "Community feedback shows..." / "Community research shows..."

**Storyteller signature phrases** (consistent across all 3 topics):
- "Imagine..." / "Picture..." / "Visualize..."
- "This transforms [X] into [Y]..."
- "[Named character] who [specific life context]..."
- Story structure markers: chapters, journeys, ceremonies, chronicles

These phrase signatures never cross-contaminate. Across 37KB of output, the Audience Advocate never constructs a named-character scenario; the Storyteller never performs a barrier analysis.

### Abstraction Level: Segments vs Characters

The Audience Advocate thinks in **user segments and demographics**: low-income residents, renters, shift workers, people with anxiety disorders, children with sensory processing differences, communities with cultural norms around sharing. Ideas are framed as policies or system designs that address segment-level barriers.

The Storyteller thinks in **specific named individuals**: Mr. and Mrs. Lopez in their third-floor apartment, Mrs. Chen who is homebound after surgery, Jamal who works the night shift, Alex with his morning coffee, Sarah who missed meditation for three days. Ideas are framed as narrative scenes that make abstract concepts emotionally concrete.

This distinction is most visible on the **tool-library equity topic**, where both personas address the same domain of community access:

- **AA on transport barriers:** "People without cars can't easily transport large tools. Consider delivery partnerships with existing services like food delivery apps during slow times, or neighborhood tool ambassadors who can bring items to people." — Identifies the barrier, proposes a structural solution.
- **ST on transport barriers:** "Mrs. Ray walking her dog and picking up a returned drill on the same route. This integrated system makes access seamless, where tool borrowing feels as natural as borrowing a book." — Illustrates the same concept through a character's daily experience, emphasizing how it *feels*.

Both arrive at "tool delivery via community members" but through entirely different cognitive processes and with different value-adds: AA surfaces the *need*, ST makes the solution *imaginable*.

### Idea Type: Functional Recommendations vs Experiential Visions

**Audience Advocate unique idea territory** (ideas no other persona in this pair raised):
- Sensory processing accommodation as an accessibility requirement, not just engagement (space-party: "sound choice zones" for varied auditory sensitivities, scent avoidance for sensory processing differences)
- Dignity-preserving payment alternatives as a design principle (tool-library: "dignity box" donations, time-based membership alternatives, no proof-of-need requirements)
- Mental health-aware interaction design (habit-tracker: adapting to cognitive variability, separating behavior from person identity, contextualizing different types of missed days)
- Governance equity structures (tool-library: voice rotation, neighborhood cluster representation, youth advisory councils)
- Power dynamic analysis (space-party: "shared leadership model rather than spotlight," explicitly noting how hero framing "might inadvertently exclude")

**Storyteller unique idea territory** (ideas no other persona in this pair raised):
- Narrative arc as structural design element (space-party: living storybook with chapters, rescue mission narrative, time travel journey)
- Ritual and ceremony creation (tool-library: membership ceremonies with hand-carved tokens, mentorship ladder ceremonies, governance storytelling circles)
- Metaphorical data visualization (habit-tracker: growth gardens, character development arcs, progress chapters that unlock gradually)
- Emotional reframing of failure (habit-tracker: missed days as "plot twists," setbacks as "Chapter 2: Disruption" in a three-chapter arc)
- Documentary/chronicle projects for institutional trust (tool-library: neighborhood tool documentaries, community tool chronicles, living history displays)
- Named-character "day in the life" walkthroughs that simulate user testing (habit-tracker: Alex's 60-second morning check-in, Ben the new dad with 30 seconds between diaper changes)

The unique idea sets barely overlap. The AA never proposes a narrative structure or ritual; the ST never performs a barrier audit or governance analysis.

### Topic Sensitivity: Volume and Engagement Patterns

The quantitative profiles (AA: 5.3KB avg, CV=14.2%; ST: 7.6KB avg, CV=23.8%) reflect a genuine structural difference rather than quality variation:

- **Tool-library equity** shows the widest gap: ST (10.9KB) vs AA (4.7KB). The equity topic gives the Storyteller rich material for character-driven narratives — each barrier becomes a character's story. The AA remains concise because its mode is structural: identify barrier → propose solution → move on.
- **Habit-tracker UX** shows convergence: ST (6.5KB) vs AA (6.2KB). The UX topic constrains the Storyteller somewhat (fewer characters to dramatize) while giving the AA more room (mental health, cognitive variability, social anxiety — rich barrier territory).
- **Space-party theme** is mid-range: ST (7.1KB) vs AA (5.9KB). Both have strong material here — the AA thinks about inclusive experience design for diverse children, the ST builds elaborate narrative party structures.

The Storyteller's higher topic sensitivity (CV=23.8% vs 14.2%) is explained by its mode: narrative elaboration scales with the richness of available character material. The AA's consistency reflects its mode: barrier analysis is similarly structured regardless of domain.

### Framework Context: Ethnographer and Empath Adjacency

The synthesis (§4.2) proposed an Ethnographer persona to fill the gap between "advocating for users" (AA) and "inhabiting the user's experience" (unarticulated needs, empathy maps, user journey pain points). The question is whether the AA/ST pair already covers this territory.

**What the Ethnographer would do that the AA doesn't:** The AA identifies barriers from an advocacy stance — it knows *what* users need. It does not simulate the experience of *being* the user. Its outputs are prescriptive ("consider X for population Y") rather than exploratory ("here is what it feels like to encounter this system as person Z").

**What the Ethnographer would do that the ST doesn't:** The Storyteller does inhabit characters' experiences, but in service of *narrative engagement* — making ideas vivid and emotionally resonant. It does not systematically map pain points, uncover unarticulated needs, or produce empathy maps. Its character scenarios are illustrative devices, not research instruments.

**Assessment:** The AA and ST together cover *parts* of the ethnographic territory — the AA covers the "what users need" half and the ST covers the "what it feels like" half — but neither performs the core ethnographic move of *discovering needs the user can't articulate*. The Ethnographer gap (§4.2) is partially mitigated by this pair but not fully closed. The Empath gap (§4.1) is largely uncovered by either — neither persona operates from raw emotional/intuitive reception without a structural or narrative framework.

## Recommendation

**Keep both.** The Audience Advocate and Storyteller are highly distinct across all three topics. They approach human-centeredness from genuinely different cognitive modes — structural equity analysis vs experiential narrative design — and produce non-overlapping idea sets. The volume difference (~40% larger for ST) is a structural feature of the Storyteller's narrative mode, not a quality differential.

**Absorb ethnographic elements into Audience Advocate prompt** (per synthesis §4.2 recommendation). The AA could be strengthened with a directive to move beyond barrier identification into active empathic exploration — mapping user journeys, surfacing unarticulated needs, and producing pain-point excavations. This would partially address the Ethnographer gap without adding a new persona, while keeping the AA distinct from the Storyteller (whose user journeys are narrative devices for engagement, not empathic research tools for need discovery).

Suggested prompt augmentation for the Audience Advocate:
- Add: "Go beyond identifying what users need — explore what they *experience*. Map the emotional journey of encountering this system. Surface needs users themselves may not be able to articulate."
- Add: "Use empathy mapping techniques: What does the user think, feel, say, and do at each stage of interaction?"
- Preserve existing barrier-identification and advocacy orientation as the primary mode.

This augmentation should be tested and compared against the current AA prompt to ensure it doesn't dilute the barrier-identification strength that makes the AA valuable.

## Notes

- **The Storyteller is the most "ethnographic" existing persona** by output pattern — its character scenarios are the closest thing in the current roster to user journey simulation. However, its ethnographic quality is incidental to its narrative purpose. A dedicated Ethnographer or AA-with-ethnographic-augmentation would perform this function deliberately.
- **The pair has complementary synthesis value.** In the synthesis phase, the AA's barrier analysis + the ST's experiential scenarios would naturally inform each other — the AA identifies who is excluded, the ST illustrates what inclusion looks like in practice. This complementarity suggests they are more valuable together than either would be alone.
- **The Storyteller's named-character technique is unique in the roster.** No other persona (across all 18 evaluated) consistently creates named characters with specific life contexts. This is a distinctive brainstorming contribution that makes abstract ideas testable ("Would this actually work for Mrs. Chen?") in a way that segment-level analysis cannot.
- **Neither persona showed signs of role drift** across the three topics. The AA never adopted narrative framing; the ST never adopted advocacy language. Prompt adherence is strong for both.
