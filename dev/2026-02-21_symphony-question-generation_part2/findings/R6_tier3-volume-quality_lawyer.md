# R6 Assessment: Lawyer

**Phase 2C Method (expected):** Synthesis
**Phase 2C Method (assessed):** Synthesis
**Recommended Volume Range:** 8-10
**Filler Onset:** ~11 questions
**Topic Sensitivity:** High

## Volume-Quality Curve

### 8-12 Questions

**Actual counts across 7 topics:** 11, 12, 12, 12, 12, 12, 12. Mean: 11.9. Mode: 12.
**Cluster counts across 7 topics:** 4, 4, 4, 4, 4, 4, 4. Mean: 4.0. Mode: 4.

The Lawyer almost universally hits 12 questions and 4 clusters at this volume level. The single exception is wearable-device at 11 questions (still 4 clusters). This extreme consistency in both count and cluster structure is the first notable finding: the Lawyer has a rigid structural template it applies across all topics.

The 4-cluster structure is remarkably stable. Across all 7 topics, the clusters follow a predictable pattern:

1. **Regulatory/compliance framework** (permits, licensing, statutory authority)
2. **Liability/risk allocation** (entity structure, insurance, vicarious liability)
3. **Contract/governance structure** (agreements, termination provisions, dispute resolution)
4. **Strategic/operational compliance** (political engagement, community positioning, enforcement defense)

The ordering and labeling vary by topic, but the underlying framework is the same. This is a highly structural persona -- it applies a fixed legal-risk-assessment framework to whatever topic it encounters.

**Quality characteristics at q08-12:**

- **Specificity is consistently high.** The Lawyer produces questions dense with specific regulatory citations, statutory frameworks, and compliance mechanisms. Youth-mentorship Q1 names FCRA disclosure requirements, Q4 distinguishes mandatory reporter status under specific state law frameworks, Q8 addresses juvenile record restrictions. Property-management Q6 identifies the tension between noise monitoring ordinances and wiretapping laws. These are not generic "what about liability?" questions -- they identify specific legal mechanisms and regulatory intersections.

- **Open-endedness is genuinely low.** This confirms the selection guide's observation. Most questions are structured as "what specific [legal mechanism] governs [situation], and what [compliance architecture] should you build?" This is a closed-frame question format that guides the brainstormer toward a specific category of answer (legal protections) rather than opening new conceptual territory. Of the 83 total q08-12 questions across 7 topics, approximately 65-70 are structured this way. Only 10-15 questions genuinely open new conceptual space (e.g., property-management Q12 on "good neighbor" operational standards as legal defense, which reframes compliance as strategic positioning rather than obligation).

- **Redundancy within a topic is low at this volume.** Each question addresses a distinct legal concern. The 4-cluster structure effectively partitions the legal landscape into non-overlapping zones. Internal redundancy does not appear until questions 11-12, where questions occasionally become a second pass at a concern already addressed (e.g., food-truck Q11 "scalable contract framework" and Q12 "exit strategy" both address long-term structural planning, with Q12 feeling like a speculative extension of Q11's practical concern).

- **Question length is extreme.** The Lawyer produces the longest questions of any persona tested. Many questions are 80-120 words, with some exceeding 150 words. This is not padding -- the length comes from stacking multiple specific sub-questions and regulatory references into a single compound question. Youth-mentorship Q7 (parental consent) is 89 words and addresses custodial authority, foster care, guardianship, non-custodial objections, and juvenile justice referral consent in a single question. This density is characteristic of the persona but creates challenges for synthesis: a single Lawyer question may embed 3-4 distinct issues that need to be disaggregated for synthesis with other personas' questions.

- **Generative expansion is minimal.** Questions almost never invite the brainstormer to imagine alternatives, envision possibilities, or explore creative solutions. The dominant mode is evaluative: "what risks exist," "what compliance obligations apply," "what protections should you build." This is a genuine limitation for Phase 2B, where the goal is to generate brainstorming questions that open creative territory. The Lawyer consistently closes territory by pre-framing it as a risk-mitigation problem.

**Strong-trigger vs. weak-trigger quality at q08-12:**

Strong-trigger topics (property-management, youth-mentorship, wearable-device) produce noticeably more specific and higher-quality questions. Youth-mentorship questions reference FCRA, FERPA, mandatory reporter statutes, and juvenile record protections with precision. Wearable-device questions navigate FDA/wellness classification, BAA structures, HIPAA applicability thresholds, and false-negative liability with genuine regulatory sophistication. Property-management questions address HOA covenant preemption, noise monitoring vs. wiretapping law tension, and occupancy tax multi-jurisdictional compliance.

Weak-trigger topics (food-truck, mobile-app, school-consolidation, career-change) still produce competent legal analysis but at lower specificity and with more generic compliance framing. Food-truck questions address entity selection, worker classification, and IP protection -- legitimate concerns but ones a Devil's Advocate could flag at a high level. Mobile-app questions about data deletion obligations and consumer protection risks are solid but heavily overlap with what any privacy-aware brainstormer would consider. School-consolidation questions are strong (statutory authority, bond covenant, union successorship) because the topic has genuine legal complexity even though Lawyer is not the primary trigger.

The career-change false-positive control is addressed in the Topic Sensitivity section below.

**Assessment at q08-12:** The Lawyer produces high-specificity, evaluative questions with a rigid 4-cluster structure. Quality is high on strong-trigger topics but the questions are consistently closed-frame (risk identification, compliance architecture) rather than open-ended (creative exploration, possibility mapping). At 12 questions, the persona is at its limit -- questions 11-12 occasionally feel like extensions of earlier concerns rather than genuinely new territory. The optimal count at this volume level is 8-10, where every question addresses a distinct, high-priority legal concern.

### 10-15 Questions

**Actual counts across 7 topics:** 12, 14, 14, 14, 15, 15, 17. Mean: 14.4. Mode: 14.
**Cluster counts across 7 topics:** 4, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 6. Mean: 4.6. Mode: 4.

**Note:** This volume level has only 1 run per topic. Conclusions should be treated as directional rather than definitive.

At q10-15, the Lawyer's behavior diverges from q08-12 in several ways:

- **Question count inflation is topic-dependent.** Wearable-device balloons to 17 questions (exceeding the 15-question ceiling), while food-truck stays at 12. This suggests the persona calibrates volume to perceived legal complexity of the topic rather than the volume target -- topics with more regulatory surface area receive more questions regardless of the target range.

- **Cluster count increases modestly but unevenly.** Food-truck jumps from 4 clusters (q08-12) to 6 clusters (q10-15) despite only producing 12 questions, creating an over-fragmented structure with only 2 questions per cluster. Career-change goes from 4 to 5 clusters. The over-clustering problem flagged in the selection guide (7-8 clusters) does not manifest at this volume for most topics (most stay at 4), but the food-truck result shows it can emerge even at moderate question counts when the persona fragments its output.

- **Filler characteristics are distinctive.** The Lawyer's filler is not vague or generic -- it takes two forms:

  1. **Regulatory granularity escalation.** The persona begins enumerating increasingly specific regulatory sub-domains that do not add conceptual value to brainstorming. Career-change Q13 (employment law classification for public interest roles) and Q14 (contractual obligations to current employer) are technically valid but address such narrow regulatory slices that they would not meaningfully inform brainstorming direction. Wearable-device Q16 (patient consent in clinical integration) and Q17 (FDA Pre-Submission strategy) are genuinely useful questions for a regulatory counsel but are too operationally specific for Phase 2B brainstorming.

  2. **Explanatory bloat.** At q10-15, several topics show the Lawyer adding multi-sentence explanatory context to each question. School-consolidation q10-15 is the most extreme example: every question includes a 3-5 sentence explanatory paragraph after the question itself. These paragraphs explain why the question matters, which is useful pedagogically but adds 40-60% to file size without adding brainstorming direction. This is the mechanism behind the selection guide's observation of "largest file sizes."

- **Quality of the core questions remains strong.** The first 10-11 questions in each q10-15 run are comparable in quality to the q08-12 runs. The filler is concentrated in questions 12+ (for topics that exceed 12) or manifests as structural fragmentation (food-truck's 6-cluster, 12-question output).

**Estimated filler at q10-15:** 15-25%. For a 14-question run, approximately 2-4 questions are regulatory granularity filler. For the 17-question wearable-device run, approximately 4-5 questions are filler. Filler onset is approximately question 11-12 regardless of the volume target.

**Assessment at q10-15 (directional, limited data):** The higher volume target does not improve question quality and introduces two filler patterns: regulatory granularity escalation and explanatory bloat. The Lawyer naturally wants to produce 12+ questions on legally complex topics regardless of target range, so the q10-15 target mainly amplifies the tendency toward operational specificity that makes questions less useful for brainstorming. The q08-12 range is better, and a tighter 8-10 target would be optimal.

## Synthesis vs Append Determination

**Assessed: Synthesis.**

The Lawyer's questions should be processed through synthesis rather than appended directly. The rationale:

1. **Heavy thematic overlap with Devil's Advocate on risk territory.** Both personas identify risks and failure modes. The Lawyer adds regulatory specificity (specific statutes, compliance frameworks, liability doctrines), while Devil's Advocate frames risks as strategic vulnerabilities and social/political dangers. These need to be cross-referenced to avoid redundant risk coverage in the synthesis output. A question about "liability for guest injuries" (Lawyer) and "what happens when a guest gets hurt and sues" (Devil's Advocate) address the same underlying concern at different levels of specificity -- synthesis can merge them into a single, well-grounded question.

2. **Legal framing is not distinctive enough to warrant preservation.** Unlike Provocateur's rhetorical provocations or Storyteller's narrative frames, the Lawyer's legal framing does not add perspective diversity that would be diluted by synthesis. The value is in the specific regulatory concerns identified, not in the framing language. Synthesis can extract the regulatory insights and integrate them with Tier 1 questions without losing substantive content.

3. **Dense compound questions need disaggregation.** Many Lawyer questions embed 3-4 sub-issues in a single question. Synthesis provides the opportunity to disaggregate these into focused questions that can be individually addressed in brainstorming. Appending would preserve the compound structure, which is harder for brainstormers to work with.

4. **Risk of cognitive overload if appended.** The Lawyer's 12 questions at q08-12 are already the densest and longest of any persona. Appending 10-12 additional high-density legal questions to a synthesis output that already includes Devil's Advocate risk coverage would overwhelm the brainstorming input with evaluative, closed-frame questions. Synthesis allows the synthesis step to select the highest-value legal concerns and integrate them at appropriate density.

**Caveat on synthesis difficulty:** The selection guide correctly flags that synthesis may struggle with the dense legal framing. The synthesizer will need to translate regulatory-specific questions into brainstorming-appropriate language without losing the specificity that is the Lawyer's primary contribution. A question about "FCRA compliance for volunteer screening" should become something like "What background check framework protects both the organization and volunteer applicants, considering FCRA disclosure requirements?" rather than either preserving the raw legal framing or stripping it to generic "what about background checks?"

## Tier 1 Overlap Assessment

### Lawyer vs. Devil's Advocate

The overlap between Lawyer and Devil's Advocate is substantial but differentiated along a clear axis: **specificity of mechanism vs. breadth of framing.**

**Areas of high overlap:**
- Both identify liability and risk exposure. Devil's Advocate asks "what could go wrong" broadly; Lawyer identifies specific legal mechanisms through which things go wrong.
- Both address governance and accountability structures. Devil's Advocate frames these as power dynamics and failure points; Lawyer frames them as compliance architecture and entity design.
- Both address stakeholder conflict. Devil's Advocate frames conflicts as political/social risks; Lawyer frames them as contractual allocation and regulatory compliance.

**Areas where Lawyer adds unique value (strong-trigger topics):**
- **Specific regulatory frameworks** that Devil's Advocate would not identify: FCRA, FERPA, mandatory reporter statutes, FDA/wellness classification, BAA structures, HIPAA applicability thresholds, wiretapping law tension with noise monitoring, bond covenant violations in consolidation, successorship doctrines for union contracts.
- **Compliance architecture design** -- not just "there's a risk" but "here's the specific compliance infrastructure you need." Devil's Advocate flags the problem; Lawyer prescribes the structural solution.
- **Jurisdictional complexity** -- multi-state, multi-authority compliance considerations that Devil's Advocate's broader framing would not surface.

**Areas where overlap makes Lawyer redundant (weak-trigger topics):**
- On food-truck, mobile-app, and career-change, the Lawyer's questions about entity structure, worker classification, IP protection, data privacy, and consumer protection risks are concerns that Devil's Advocate (and to some extent Analyst) already covers at sufficient depth for brainstorming purposes. The Lawyer adds regulatory citations but not conceptual territory.
- On school-consolidation, the overlap is more nuanced: Devil's Advocate would flag the political risks of consolidation, while Lawyer adds genuine value on statutory authority, bond covenant, and labor law -- but these are also partially covered by Politician. The Lawyer's unique contribution is narrower here.

**Overlap severity:** On strong-trigger topics, the overlap is productive (Lawyer deepens Devil's Advocate's risk identification with specific mechanisms). On weak-trigger topics, the overlap is redundant (Lawyer restates Devil's Advocate territory with legal jargon but without new conceptual territory). This reinforces the topic-gated inclusion model.

## Topic Sensitivity Analysis

### Strong-Trigger Topics

**Property-management (q08-12, q10-15):** The Lawyer's strongest showing. Questions address specific regulatory intersections that no other persona would identify: noise monitoring vs. wiretapping law (Q6), occupancy tax compliance across jurisdictions with different tax bases (Q5), HOA covenant preemption vs. city permit conflicts (Q10), political advocacy disclosure requirements for property owner anonymity (Q11). At q08-12, all 12 questions are high-value. At q10-15 (14 questions), the additional 2 questions (data privacy, political engagement) are solid but begin to overlap with Tier 1 coverage. This is the persona's best-case performance: a topic with dense, multi-layered regulatory landscape where the Lawyer identifies specific mechanisms and intersections.

**Youth-mentorship (q08-12, q10-15):** Nearly as strong as property-management. The combination of mandatory reporting, juvenile justice data privacy, FERPA, FCRA, parental consent complexity (custodial vs. non-custodial), and faith-based organization liability creates a regulatory landscape that only the Lawyer can navigate with specificity. Q7 (parental consent with complex family situations) and Q8 (juvenile justice data privacy) are questions that no Tier 1 persona would generate. At q10-15 (14 questions), the additional questions address volunteer legal status/employment law boundaries (Q12), IRB requirements for evaluation (Q13), and communication monitoring (Q14) -- these are useful but increasingly operational.

**Wearable-device (q08-12, q10-15):** Strong performance on the implicit trigger. The Lawyer navigates the wellness-vs-medical-device classification with genuine sophistication (Q1 on intended use language, Q2 on pre-submission strategy), addresses state-level medical data regulations beyond HIPAA (Q3), and surfaces the false-negative liability problem specific to health monitoring devices (Q7). At q08-12 (11 questions), the output is tight and high-value. At q10-15, the persona overproduces to 17 questions, with questions 13-17 becoming increasingly specific to clinical partnership governance and FDA pre-submission procedures -- useful for a regulatory attorney but too operationally specific for brainstorming.

### Weak-Trigger Topics

**Food-truck (q08-12, q10-15):** Competent but generic. Questions about entity structure (Q4), worker classification (Q5), IP protection (Q9), and SBA loan covenants (Q7) are legitimate but are concerns a Devil's Advocate or Analyst could flag at sufficient depth. The most distinctive question is Q2 (commissary contract protections and food safety liability from shared facilities), which identifies a specific regulatory dependency. At q10-15 (12 questions, 6 clusters), the over-fragmentation problem emerges: 6 clusters with 2 questions each creates a scattered structure that suggests the persona is searching for legal territory rather than responding to genuine regulatory complexity.

**Mobile-app (q08-12, q10-15):** Surprisingly strong for a weak-trigger topic. The Lawyer identifies several non-obvious legal dimensions of app simplification: data retention obligations for removed features (Q1), contractual obligations surviving feature removal (Q2), app store "bait-and-switch" policy risks (Q7), and employment law implications of team restructuring (Q12). However, the density of data-privacy and consumer-protection questions creates redundancy -- Q1, Q3, Q6, and Q11 all address variations of "what happens to the data when you remove features." At q10-15, this redundancy worsens. The mobile-app topic reveals the Lawyer's tendency to over-index on data privacy as a default territory when the topic lacks obvious regulatory triggers.

**School-consolidation (q08-12, q10-15):** Above average for a non-primary trigger. The topic has genuine legal complexity (statutory authority, bond covenants, union successorship, property tax equalization) even though Politician is the primary trigger. Questions about voter approval thresholds and defeat consequences (Q2), existing bond obligations (Q4), and union contract reconciliation (Q7) add value that Politician would not provide. At q10-15 (14 questions), the explanatory bloat problem is most severe here: each question gets a 3-5 sentence explanatory paragraph that inflates file size without adding brainstorming direction.

### Career-Change Control

**Assessment: Partial false positive.** The career-change topic is the deliberate trap -- it involves "becoming a lawyer" but the brainstorming need is personal career decision-making. The Lawyer's response is instructive:

At q08-12 (12 questions, 4 clusters), the Lawyer produces a set of questions that are technically competent but largely misaligned with the user's actual brainstorming need. The four clusters are: Regulatory Authorization & Professional Licensure, Financial Structure & Debt Regulation, Professional Practice & Compliance Architecture, and Risk Assessment & Structural Protections.

The first three clusters (Q1-Q9) are essentially a regulatory roadmap to becoming a public interest immigration lawyer. Questions about bar admission prerequisites (Q1), EOIR accreditation requirements (Q7), and professional responsibility rules for immigrant advocacy (Q9) are useful information for someone who has already decided to become a lawyer, but they do not help the user think through whether this career change makes sense. They assume the decision and focus on implementation compliance.

The fourth cluster (Q10-Q12) contains the only genuinely useful questions for the brainstorming need: Q10 asks about structural risk mitigation (part-time law school, trial period through volunteer work), Q11 addresses jurisdictional mobility planning, and Q12 analyzes alternative pathways to immigrant advocacy without a full JD. These questions engage with the actual decision rather than the regulatory implementation.

This yields approximately 25% useful questions (3 of 12) for the brainstorming need, compared to 80-90% useful questions on strong-trigger topics. The Lawyer responds to the keyword "lawyer" and produces a regulatory implementation guide rather than brainstorming questions about the career transition decision.

At q10-15 (15 questions, 5 clusters), the pattern intensifies. The additional questions deepen the regulatory roadmap (DOJ recognition, EOIR accreditation, immigration court procedures, nonprofit governance, HIPAA considerations for medical-legal partnership) without adding any questions about the actual career decision. The useful question percentage drops to approximately 20% (3 of 15).

**Verdict:** The career-change control confirms that the Lawyer is keyword-triggered, not need-triggered. The orchestrator should not select the Lawyer based on topic keywords (e.g., "law school," "legal") but only when the brainstorming need involves navigating legal/regulatory exposure. This is a genuine false-positive scenario that the orchestrator selection logic should be designed to avoid.

### Topic Sensitivity Verdict

**Topic sensitivity: High.** The quality gap between strong-trigger and weak-trigger topics is among the largest of any persona tested.

- **Strong-trigger topics** (property-management, youth-mentorship, wearable-device): 80-95% of questions add unique value that no Tier 1 persona would generate. Questions identify specific regulatory mechanisms, jurisdictional intersections, and compliance architectures that are critical for the topic.
- **Weak-trigger topics** (food-truck, school-consolidation): 40-60% of questions add unique value. The remainder overlaps with Devil's Advocate or Analyst territory at a level of regulatory specificity that adds jargon but not conceptual territory.
- **Mobile-app:** An interesting middle case at ~50-60% useful, primarily because data privacy and consumer protection create a secondary regulatory dimension that the Lawyer can address, though with redundancy.
- **Career-change control:** ~20-25% useful. The Lawyer responds to keywords rather than brainstorming need, producing a regulatory implementation guide instead of decision-exploration questions.

The quality gap between strong-trigger (85-95% useful) and career-change control (20-25% useful) is approximately 60-70 percentage points. This is a decisive gap that confirms the persona should be strictly topic-gated.

**Volume should not vary by topic type.** The optimal volume (8-10) applies across all topic types. On strong-trigger topics, 8-10 questions contain the persona's core regulatory insights; questions 11-12 are still useful but with diminishing returns. On weak-trigger topics, a lower count would reduce redundancy with Tier 1, but the orchestrator should simply not select the Lawyer for weak-trigger topics. If selected (presumably because the orchestrator identified a regulatory dimension), 8-10 questions is sufficient.

## Filler Detection

### q08-12 Filler Analysis

**Estimated filler at q08-12:** 10-15% (approximately 1-2 questions per 12-question set).

Filler at this volume level is concentrated at questions 11-12 and takes two forms:

1. **Speculative structural planning.** Questions about exit strategy (food-truck Q12), scalable contract frameworks (food-truck Q11), and credential portability (career-change Q11) address real concerns but are speculative extensions that feel like "what else can I add?" rather than responses to regulatory urgency. These questions are not bad, but they add less value than questions 1-10.

2. **Over-specificity on narrow regulatory slices.** Income protection & insurance structuring (career-change Q6) is a legitimate but extremely narrow concern for a brainstorming session about whether to change careers. Food safety compliance architecture (food-truck Q6) is detailed to the point of being a compliance checklist rather than a brainstorming question.

**Filler is less severe on strong-trigger topics.** Property-management and youth-mentorship have zero filler questions at 12 -- every question addresses a distinct, high-priority legal concern. Wearable-device at 11 questions is similarly tight. Filler is concentrated on weak-trigger topics where the persona is searching for legal territory.

### q10-15 Filler Analysis (limited data)

**Estimated filler at q10-15:** 15-25%, concentrated at questions 12+.

Filler characteristics at this volume:

1. **Regulatory granularity escalation.** The persona begins enumerating sub-regulatory domains that do not add brainstorming value. Career-change Q13 (FLSA exemptions for salaried professionals) and Q14 (contractual obligations to current employer) are noise for a career-decision brainstorming session. Wearable-device Q16 (patient consent in clinical integration) and Q17 (FDA Pre-Submission strategy) are operationally specific guidance better suited for a regulatory counsel than a brainstorming session.

2. **Explanatory bloat.** School-consolidation q10-15 adds 3-5 sentence explanatory paragraphs after each question, roughly doubling file size without adding brainstorming direction. This is the most consistent filler pattern at q10-15.

3. **Over-clustering.** Food-truck q10-15 produces 12 questions across 6 clusters (vs. 4 clusters at q08-12), with 2 questions per cluster. The persona fragments its output into too many micro-categories rather than deepening existing clusters.

**Filler onset: approximately question 11.** The first 10 questions are consistently high-quality across both volume levels and all topic types. Questions 11-12 are variable (strong on high-affinity topics, filler on low-affinity topics). Questions 13+ are predominantly filler.

## Cross-Topic Consistency

The Lawyer's general quality characteristics are remarkably consistent across all 7 topics:

1. **Cluster structure is invariant at 4.** At q08-12, every single topic produces exactly 4 clusters. This is the most rigid cluster structure of any persona in the test set.

2. **Question density (word count) is consistently high.** Questions average 80-120 words across all topics, with compound sub-question structures throughout.

3. **Evaluative/closed-frame orientation is constant.** Every topic receives the same "what risks exist / what compliance architecture is needed" framing. The persona never produces open-ended, generative, or appreciative questions on any topic.

4. **Specificity varies with topic affinity** but the structural approach is identical. On weak-trigger topics, the persona applies the same 4-cluster, evaluative framework but fills it with more generic legal concerns. On strong-trigger topics, it fills the same framework with highly specific regulatory mechanisms.

5. **No dimensional drift.** The Lawyer never strays into non-legal territory. Unlike some personas that shift voice or focus at different volumes, the Lawyer stays exclusively within legal/regulatory/compliance framing across all topics and volumes.

This consistency means the persona is predictable: the orchestrator can expect exactly 4 clusters of evaluative, compliance-oriented questions at any volume. The variable is specificity of content (which depends on topic affinity), not structure or approach.

## Summary Recommendation

**Recommended volume range: 8-10 questions.** The Lawyer produces its highest-value questions in the first 10, with filler emerging at question 11-12 on most topics. A target of 8-10 allows the persona to cover its core regulatory insights without padding.

**Phase 2C method: Synthesis.** The Lawyer's questions overlap substantially with Devil's Advocate on risk territory and need to be cross-referenced to avoid redundant risk coverage. The legal framing is not distinctive enough to warrant preservation through Append. However, the synthesis step must be careful to preserve the regulatory specificity that is the Lawyer's primary contribution -- stripping legal citations would eliminate the persona's unique value.

**Topic sensitivity: High.** The Lawyer should only be selected when the topic involves genuine legal/regulatory exposure -- liability, zoning, compliance, data privacy with regulatory frameworks, regulatory classification. The orchestrator should not trigger on domain keywords (e.g., "law school," "legal career") but on the presence of regulatory/compliance concerns in the brainstorming need itself.

**Topic-gating guidance for the orchestrator:**
- **Select when:** The topic involves liability exposure, regulatory compliance, zoning/permitting, data privacy with specific regulatory frameworks (HIPAA, FERPA, GDPR), entity structure for asset protection, or multi-jurisdictional regulatory navigation.
- **Do not select when:** The topic mentions legal concepts but the brainstorming need is personal/strategic rather than regulatory (e.g., career change to law, legal strategy for a personal decision). Let Devil's Advocate handle high-level risk identification in these cases.

**Caveats:**
- The q10-15 data is thin (1 run per topic). The filler onset estimate of ~11 questions and the specific filler patterns (regulatory granularity escalation, explanatory bloat, over-clustering) should be verified with additional runs if Tier 3 volume calibration becomes a priority.
- The Lawyer's extreme question density (80-120 words per question, compound sub-questions) may create challenges for synthesis. The synthesizer will need to disaggregate compound questions and translate regulatory language into brainstorming-appropriate framing. This is a synthesis difficulty issue, not a reason to switch to Append.
- The selection guide's warning about "7-8 clusters" was not confirmed at the tested volumes (4 clusters was universal at q08-12; maximum 6 clusters at q10-15). The over-clustering problem may emerge at higher volumes (15+) or may have been observed with different prompt structures. At the recommended 8-10 volume, 4 clusters is expected and manageable.
