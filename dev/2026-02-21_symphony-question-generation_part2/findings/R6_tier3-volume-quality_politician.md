## Synthesis vs Append Determination

**Assessed: Append.**

The selection guide expected Synthesis. After reviewing 14 test runs across 7 topics, the assessment is that the Politician should use the Append method instead. The rationale:

**1. Distinctive framing that synthesis would dilute.** The Politician's questions are not just "political angle on Topic X." They employ a specific analytical vocabulary -- veto players, coalition sequencing, political capital, governance structure, stakeholder mapping, opposition management -- that represents a genuinely distinctive way of seeing problems. Synthesis would strip this framing and reduce the questions to their thematic content, which would make them indistinguishable from Audience Advocate or Devil's Advocate questions about stakeholders and opposition.

Consider school-consolidation Q1: "What are the minimum vote thresholds and coalition compositions needed in each district to pass consolidation, and which stakeholder groups in each district represent the swing voters who could deliver or block those majorities?" In synthesis, this becomes something like "What voter support is needed for consolidation?" -- a dramatically less useful question. The Politician's value is precisely in the strategic specificity of the framing.

**2. Low thematic overlap with Tier 1 at the question level.** Despite surface-level overlap on topics like "stakeholders" and "opposition" (shared with Audience Advocate and Devil's Advocate), the Politician's questions operate at a different level of analysis. Audience Advocate asks "who is affected and what do they need?" Devil's Advocate asks "what could go wrong?" The Politician asks "who holds power, what do they want, and what sequence of engagement changes the outcome?" These are different enough that synthesis would lose more than it gains.

**3. The Politician already self-synthesizes internally.** The persona's questions are organized into well-structured clusters (4 per topic at q08-12) that function as a coherent strategic framework. Each cluster addresses a different dimension of political analysis (regulatory strategy, coalition building, opposition management, governance structure). Synthesis with Tier 1 questions would break this internal structure.

**Risk of Append:** The Politician's questions are long (many are 50-100+ words). In Append mode, the total question volume for a topic could increase substantially. This is manageable if the Politician's volume is capped at 10 questions, which provides its core contribution without the verbose tail.

## Tier 1 Overlap Assessment

### Politician vs Audience Advocate

**Surface overlap:** Both personas address stakeholder concerns. The Audience Advocate asks "who is affected and what do they need?" while the Politician asks "who holds power and what sequence of engagement changes outcomes?"

**Actual overlap is low.** The Audience Advocate centers the user/beneficiary perspective and asks empathetic questions about needs, access, and experience. The Politician centers the decision-maker perspective and asks strategic questions about power, coalitions, and governance. On youth-mentorship, the Audience Advocate would ask "What do at-risk teens need from a mentor relationship?" The Politician asks "Which community leaders hold informal authority over youth programming, and how do we secure their endorsement before launch?" (Q1, q10-15). These are complementary, not overlapping.

The closest overlap occurs on topics with explicit community dimensions (property-management, school-consolidation), where both personas address "community trust." But even here, the framing differs: the Audience Advocate asks about trust as an emotional state to be built, while the Politician asks about trust as political capital to be invested.

**Estimated thematic convergence: 10-15%.** Mostly on community-facing topics where "stakeholder" language overlaps.

### Politician vs Devil's Advocate

**Surface overlap:** Both personas address opposition and risk. Devil's Advocate asks "what could go wrong?" while the Politician asks "who will oppose this and how do you neutralize them?"

**Actual overlap is moderate but functionally distinct.** Devil's Advocate challenges assumptions and stress-tests plans from a contrarian position. The Politician maps opposition as a strategic landscape to be navigated. On food-truck, Devil's Advocate might ask "What if the fusion concept doesn't differentiate in a market with 4 similar trucks?" The Politician asks "How might the established brick-and-mortar restaurant association view food truck expansion, and can their opposition be neutralized or converted?" (Q3, q08-12). The Devil's Advocate question challenges the business premise; the Politician question maps the political landscape of institutional opposition.

The closest overlap is on "opposition management" -- both personas identify who might oppose a plan. But the Devil's Advocate focuses on whether the opposition is *right* (stress-testing the plan), while the Politician focuses on how to *manage* the opposition (strategic navigation). These produce different downstream brainstorming value.

**Estimated thematic convergence: 15-20%.** Concentrated in the opposition/risk territory, but with distinct analytical postures.

### Original Topics — Selection Threshold Validation

Three topics from the original test suite were assessed using existing q15-20 data (generated 2026-02-18 with an earlier prompt) to validate the Politician's selection threshold. These runs were not part of the R6 test data but provide useful signal for calibrating when the orchestrator should and should not select the Politician.

**Tool-library (18 Qs, q15-20): Moderate trigger.**

The political-analytical lens is fully activated. The distinctive vocabulary is present throughout — coalition cascade ("What sequence of stakeholder endorsements creates the most powerful coalition cascade?"), political capital ("What political capital is required to secure in-kind donations?"), founder lock as a power dynamics concept (Q5 on governance capture, Q14 on volunteer power-sharing), political legitimacy thresholds ("What threshold of community participation is required for political legitimacy?"), veto power mapping ("Who has the power to say yes, and who has the power to say no?"), opposition typology (Q9: "principled disagreement, territorial threat, resource competition, or inertia"), and organizational capture vs political abandonment (Q20).

**Assessment:** Should trigger at High effort (moderate+ threshold) but not Medium (strong trigger only). The political dimension is real but secondary — the topic's primary brainstorming need is operational (how to run a tool library), with governance/stakeholder complexity as a meaningful supporting dimension.

**Habit-tracker (17 Qs, q15-20): Not a trigger.**

The Politician maps its framework onto the topic but the distinctive political vocabulary is largely absent. **Space-party (16 Qs, q15-20): Not a trigger.**

The political vocabulary is present but disproportionate to the context. ### Topic Sensitivity Verdict

**Topic sensitivity is real but more nuanced than the selection guide suggested.** The Politician does not "double output for institutional topics." Rather, the persona produces 10-12 questions of high quality across *all* tested topics, with the quality gap manifesting not in volume but in:

1. **Depth ceiling.** On strong-trigger topics, the persona can sustain quality to 12 questions with near-zero filler. On weak-trigger topics, quality begins to degrade around question 10-11, with the career-change control showing the lowest ceiling.

2. **Domain authenticity.** On strong-trigger topics, every question operates in the Politician's native domain (governance, coalitions, regulatory strategy). On weak-trigger topics, the persona must translate its framework to organizational dynamics and decision-making governance -- still valuable, but one degree removed from its core competence.

3. **Filler onset gap.** Strong-trigger topics: filler onset at ~12 questions. Weak-trigger topics: filler onset at ~10-11 questions. Career-change control: filler onset at ~10 questions. This is a real but modest gap (2-3 questions).

**The quality gap between strong and weak triggers is smaller than expected.** The Politician is not a persona that produces garbage on off-trigger topics. It produces genuinely useful organizational-political analysis for any topic with multiple stakeholders, institutional complexity, or governance decisions -- which is most real-world brainstorming topics. The question is not "should the Politician be deployed on weak-trigger topics" but "does the Politician add enough marginal value on weak-trigger topics to justify the additional question volume in synthesis?"

**Recommendation:** Volume targets should not vary by topic type. The recommended range of 8-10 questions works well across all topics. The decision to deploy the Politician should be topic-gated (only when stakeholder politics, governance, or regulatory dynamics are central), but once deployed, the same volume target applies.

## Cross-Topic Consistency

Beyond the topic sensitivity dynamics analyzed above, the Politician demonstrates several consistent quality characteristics across all 7 topics:

**1. Consistent cluster structure.** All 14 test runs produce exactly 4 clusters (except three q10-15 runs that produce 5). The clusters map to a stable political-analysis framework that varies in label but not in function:
- Cluster 1: Regulatory/institutional strategy (who controls the rules)
- Cluster 2: Coalition building and stakeholder alignment (who are allies vs. opponents)
- Cluster 3: Governance and decision-making authority (how power is structured)
- Cluster 4: Opposition management and risk (what threatens the plan)

This framework is remarkably stable across all topic types, from school consolidation to mobile app to career change. The Politician applies the same analytical lens consistently and productively.

**2. Consistent question length.** The Politician produces the longest questions of any persona tested. At q08-12, most questions are 40-80 words. At q10-15, many expand to 80-150 words. The longer questions are not padding -- they tend to include specific stakeholder identification, concrete scenarios, and multi-part strategic framing. However, the verbosity means that 10 Politician questions contain roughly as much content as 15 questions from more concise personas (Analyst, Questioner).

**3. Consistent political vocabulary.** The persona reliably employs a distinctive vocabulary across all topics: "veto power," "coalition," "political capital," "gatekeeper," "governance structure," "opposition," "stakeholder map," "credibility," "legitimacy," "constituency." This vocabulary is not decorative -- it reflects a genuine analytical framework that produces different questions than any Tier 1 persona would generate.

**4. Consistent avoidance of financial/legal analysis.** Despite many topics having financial or legal dimensions (food-truck, property-management, wearable-device), the Politician consistently frames these in political terms (who controls funding, what does the loan relationship create, how does liability allocation affect coalitions) rather than attempting the financial or legal analysis itself. This discipline is a strength -- the persona stays in its lane.

## Summary Recommendation

**Volume range: 8-10 questions.** The Politician produces near-zero filler at 10 questions across all topics and topic types. At 12, filler begins on weak-trigger topics. The persona's questions are substantially longer than average (40-80 words each at q08-12), so 10 Politician questions deliver content volume comparable to 14-15 questions from more concise personas. A range of 8-10 maximizes quality while avoiding filler onset.

**Phase 2C method: Append.** The Politician's analytical framing (stakeholder mapping, coalition dynamics, power analysis, governance structure) is distinctive enough that synthesis would dilute its primary value. The political vocabulary and strategic specificity of the questions constitute the contribution; stripping them into thematic summaries would produce questions indistinguishable from Audience Advocate or Devil's Advocate output. Append preserves the Politician's unique value.

**Topic sensitivity: High, but differently than expected.** The Politician is not topic-gated in the binary sense (produces garbage on off-trigger topics). It produces genuinely useful organizational-political analysis for any topic with stakeholder complexity. However, its depth ceiling varies: 12 high-quality questions on strong-trigger topics (governance, regulatory, civic), 10 on weak-trigger topics. The orchestrator should deploy the Politician when stakeholder politics, governance structures, or regulatory dynamics are explicitly central to the topic. It should not be deployed for purely personal or purely technical topics where the political lens adds insufficient marginal value over Tier 1 personas.

**Caveats:**
1. q10-15 data is from a single test run per topic. Filler estimates and volume ceiling conclusions for this range should be validated with additional runs before being treated as definitive.
2. Three original topics (habit-tracker, space-party, tool-library) were assessed using q15-20 data from an earlier prompt. Tool-library was confirmed as a moderate trigger; habit-tracker and space-party were confirmed as non-triggers. This data validates selection threshold calibration but uses a different prompt and volume level than the primary R6 test data.
3. The persona's verbosity means that Append mode will increase total question volume significantly. If the question synthesis budget is constrained, the orchestrator may need to set a lower volume target (8 questions) to keep the Append contribution manageable.
