# R6 Assessment: Simplifier

**Phase 2C Method (expected):** Synthesis
**Phase 2C Method (assessed):** Exclude (fully, no conditional retention)
**Recommended Volume Range:** N/A (excluded from Phase 2B)
**Filler Onset:** ~9 questions
**Topic Sensitivity:** High

## Volume-Quality Curve

### 8-12 Questions

Across the 7 topics at the q08-12 volume level, the Simplifier produced between 10 and 12 questions per topic (career-change: 12, food-truck: 10, mobile-app: 12, property-management: 10, school-consolidation: 12, wearable-device: 12, youth-mentorship: 11). Cluster counts were remarkably stable at 3-4 per topic, confirming the selection guide's observation of consistent 4-5 cluster structure.

**Quality at 8-10 questions:** The first 8-10 questions in each set are generally strong within the Simplifier's narrow mode. The persona's core move — "what if we removed X?" — produces genuinely thought-provoking questions when applied to the most obvious assumptions in a topic. Examples of high-quality questions at this range:

- Food-truck Q1: "What if the menu had exactly three items?" — Forces a concrete constraint that reframes the entire operational model.
- Mobile-app Q1: "What if the entire app did only one thing?" — Direct, powerful reframing that aligns perfectly with the topic's stated goal.
- School-consolidation Q3: "What if we kept three boards but made them share one employee?" — A genuinely creative structural alternative that opens productive space.
- Wearable-device Q4: "Could a rules-based system replace ML entirely?" — Challenges the fundamental technical premise.

These early questions share several quality markers: they are concrete rather than abstract, they propose a specific removal or reduction that can be evaluated, and they open space for further inquiry even though their framing is convergent.

**Quality degradation at 10-12 questions:** By Q10-12, clear filler patterns emerge across all topics:

1. **The "one-sentence / one-thing" reduction formula:** Nearly every 12-question set contains a variant of "What's the one-sentence version?" or "What's the one thing you actually want?" (career-change Q10, youth-mentorship Q1 at q10-15, wearable Q9 at q10-15). This is a genuine Simplifier move at first use but becomes formulaic by the third or fourth application.

2. **Recursive removal of the same variable from different angles:** Career-change Q7 ("Is law actually the lever you think it is?"), Q8 ("What if 'calling' is just exhaustion looking for an exit?"), and Q9 ("What parts of 'lawyer' are you romanticizing?") all challenge the same assumption — that law school is the right path — from slightly different emotional angles. The first is incisive; by the third, it reads as piling on rather than opening new ground.

3. **"What if we cut the timeline in half?" variants:** This appears across multiple topics (career-change Q12, food-truck implicit in expansion questions, youth-mentorship on commitment length). It is a generic simplification move that adds limited topic-specific value.

4. **Meta-questions that close rather than open:** Questions like "What's the one-sentence version of why this makes sense?" (career-change Q10) and "What is the single essential action you want to take daily?" (career-change Q12 at q10-15) are not brainstorming questions at all — they are convergent exercises that ask the user to narrow their thinking. These belong in Phase 3 synthesis, not Phase 2B generation.

**Estimated filler at q08-12:** 15-25% across topics. In 10-question sets (food-truck, property-management), filler is around 10-15% (1-2 questions). In 12-question sets, filler rises to 20-30% (2-4 questions). The sharpest sets are the 10-question outputs, suggesting the natural ceiling is around 8-10 questions before the persona begins recycling its core moves.

### 10-15 Questions

**Data limitation:** Only 1 run per topic at this volume level. Conclusions should be treated as directional, not definitive.

At q10-15, the Simplifier produced between 12 and 15 questions (career-change: 12, food-truck: 14, mobile-app: 15, property-management: 15, school-consolidation: 15, wearable-device: 12, youth-mentorship: 13). Cluster counts expanded modestly to 4 per topic in most cases.

**Quality assessment:** The degradation pattern that begins at Q10-12 in the lower volume range becomes pronounced at Q13-15. Specific patterns:

1. **Near-duplicate questions across volume levels:** Comparing q08-12 and q10-15 runs for the same topic reveals substantial overlap. Food-truck produces "What if the menu had exactly three items?" at both levels (Q1 in both). Career-change produces overlapping "remove the law school assumption" questions across both runs. This suggests the persona has a relatively small repertoire of genuine insights per topic, and higher volume simply forces recycling.

2. **Granular decomposition of a single insight:** At q10-15, the Simplifier applies its removal lens to increasingly specific sub-domains. Food-truck at q10-15 asks about the beverage program (Q4), about one equipment decision (Q12), about one day off per week (Q13), and about ignoring competitors (Q14). These are not bad questions individually, but they represent the same cognitive move ("what if we removed/simplified X?") applied to progressively less significant targets.

3. **Cluster 4 weakness:** The fourth cluster in q10-15 sets is consistently the weakest. Food-truck's "Planning and Decision Reduction" cluster (Q12-14) and school-consolidation's "Entire Categories of Work to Eliminate" cluster (Q12-15) contain the most formulaic and least topic-specific questions.

4. **Mobile-app as ceiling test:** On its strong-trigger topic, the Simplifier reaches 15 questions. Even here, Q13 (selling features to other apps), Q14 (one notification type), and Q15 ("what if the simplest version has no features at all — just a very good question asked once per day?") show diminishing returns. Q15 in particular is clever but not productive — it is a reductio ad absurdum that closes inquiry rather than opening it.

**Estimated filler at q10-15:** 25-40%. At 15 questions, roughly 4-6 are filler. At 12 questions (career-change, wearable-device), the filler rate is closer to 20-25%, which aligns with the q08-12 observation that 10-12 is the natural boundary.

## Synthesis vs Append Determination

**Assessed: Neither — Exclude is recommended (see Phase 2B Viability Assessment below).**

If the Simplifier were retained, Synthesis would be the correct method. The rationale:

- **No distinctive framing that would be diluted by synthesis.** The Simplifier's questions use straightforward interrogative language ("What if we removed...?", "What happens if we eliminated...?"). There is no metaphorical, narrative, provocative, or paradigmatic framing that merges poorly with other questions. The subtractive lens is a content orientation, not a stylistic one.

- **High thematic overlap with Tier 1 territory.** Many Simplifier questions challenge the same assumptions that the Analyst and Devil's Advocate already probe, just with an explicit subtraction frame. Synthesis would allow cross-referencing with those overlapping questions rather than creating redundancy through append.

- **Questions are evaluative, not generative.** Append is designed for questions with distinctive generative framing (Provocateur's provocation, Storyteller's narrative reframing, Analogist's cross-domain mapping). The Simplifier's questions are evaluative — they assess whether something should exist, not what else might exist. Evaluative questions integrate naturally into synthesis.

However, the deeper issue is not Synthesis vs Append but whether the output warrants inclusion at all. See the Phase 2B Viability Assessment below.

## Tier 1 Overlap Assessment

### Simplifier vs. Analyst (Convergent Evaluation)

The overlap is substantial — estimated at 50-60%, the highest of any Tier 3/Tier 1 pair in this assessment.

**Overlapping territory:**

- Both challenge whether proposed scope is justified by evidence (Analyst asks "what data supports this?"; Simplifier asks "what if we removed this?")
- Both question growth/expansion assumptions (Analyst examines unit economics of scaling; Simplifier asks "what if we never expanded?")
- Both probe operational complexity (Analyst maps dependency chains; Simplifier asks "which dependencies could we eliminate?")
- Both evaluate whether features/components earn their place (Analyst via metrics; Simplifier via removal thought experiments)

On topics like food-truck, property-management, and school-consolidation, both personas converge on the same territory: questioning whether planned scope is justified, challenging growth assumptions, and probing which elements could be cut. The Analyst does this through systematic evaluation; the Simplifier does it through subtraction hypotheticals. The surface framing differs but the underlying inquiry direction is the same.

**Distinctive Simplifier territory (~25-30%):**

- The "what if we didn't do this at all?" question type — a more radical version of the Analyst's "is this worth doing?" The Analyst evaluates; the Simplifier proposes not doing.
- Explicit constraint-as-feature framing: "What if the constraint forced better decisions?" (food-truck Q5 on refusing debt, wearable Q7 on removing the display). The Analyst would note the constraint; the Simplifier reframes it as desirable.
- The subtractive reductio: "What if the app had no features at all?" This is a thought experiment the Analyst would not generate because it is not analytically productive. Whether it is brainstorming-productive is debatable.

**Distinctive Analyst territory (not covered by Simplifier):**

- Systematic coverage of all problem dimensions
- Quantitative framing (metrics, benchmarks, ratios)
- Stakeholder analysis and dependency mapping
- Process evaluation and timeline analysis
- Risk assessment grounded in evidence

The Simplifier's distinctive contribution is narrow: roughly one-quarter to one-third of its output covers territory not already handled by the Analyst. Of that unique territory, much consists of radical removal proposals that may be more useful as Phase 3 provocations than Phase 2B brainstorming questions.

## Topic Sensitivity Analysis

### Strong-Trigger Topic: mobile-app

The Simplifier is at its absolute best on the mobile-app topic. This is unsurprising: the topic's stated goal is literally "strip it down to its essential core," which is the Simplifier's entire cognitive mode. At q08-12, all 12 questions are well-targeted and specific:

- Q1-4 (Identifying the Essential Core) are strong: each proposes a concrete simplification lens applied to the app's specific metrics and user data.
- Q5-8 (Psychology and Practice of Removal) explore the meta-question of how to execute simplification, which is genuinely useful territory.
- Q9-12 (Relaunch as Radical Subtraction) are more uneven — Q9 (marketing "now with less") is creative, but Q11 (new product identity) and Q12 (runway-dictated scope) are more generic.

At q10-15, the 15-question set is the strongest of any topic, with genuine depth through Q12 and only the final 3 questions (Q13-15) showing clear filler. The mobile-app output demonstrates that the Simplifier can produce 10-12 high-quality questions when the topic is a perfect match.

**Quality markers on strong-trigger topic:** Questions are specific to the app's actual data (47-second sessions, 2% DAU/MAU, 6 tabs + hamburger menu), propose concrete reductions rather than abstract ones, and several open genuinely productive lines of inquiry (per-feature retention curves, willingness-to-pay-to-keep as a feature evaluation method).

### Weak-Trigger Topics

**Food-truck (no Simplifier trigger):** Solid but generic. The "three items on the menu" and "one revenue stream" questions are reasonable but could have been generated by any persona with a constraint-application lens. The persona defaults to its standard moves (what if we never expanded, what if we avoided regulatory complexity) without much topic-specific insight. Questions 7-10 at q08-12 are weaker — "what if you ignored the pending ordinance?" and "what if you cooked everything in advance?" are practical but not the kind of open-ended brainstorming questions Phase 2B targets.

**Property-management (no Simplifier trigger):** Average quality. The Simplifier defaults to its "what if the business was smaller?" lens, which produces reasonable but unsurprising questions. Q1-3 at q08-12 (scope reduction, regulatory avoidance, growth questioning) are its strongest; Q7-10 (political non-engagement, neighbor simplification, contract simplification) are more formulaic. At q10-15, Q4 ("Does 'full-service' serve customers, or the founders' desire to feel comprehensive?") is a standout — it is the kind of question that justifies the persona's existence. But it is surrounded by weaker material.

**School-consolidation (no Simplifier trigger):** Surprisingly strong. The Simplifier's "what if we didn't consolidate but instead shared services?" line of questioning (Q1-3 at q08-12, Q4-5 at q10-15) is genuinely useful and not well-covered by the Politician or Accountant triggers. Q6 at q08-12 ("What makes anyone think a three-district simultaneous vote can pass?") is an excellent meta-question. Q12 at q08-12 ("What if the state funding formula is the problem, not the solution?") reframes the entire premise productively.

**Wearable-device (no Simplifier trigger):** Good technical simplification but limited generative value. Questions like "Could a single sensor replace three?" and "Could a rules-based system replace ML entirely?" are sharp, but they are engineering evaluation questions more than brainstorming questions. They propose specific technical simplifications that have determinate answers — an engineer can evaluate whether rules-based detection works. This is Phase 3 territory, not Phase 2B.

**Youth-mentorship (no Simplifier trigger):** Mixed quality with concerning edge cases. The Simplifier's "reduce overhead" lens produces some useful questions (Q1 on commitment length, Q2 on single target audience) but also some potentially irresponsible ones. Q5 ("Which safety protocols exist out of fear rather than actual risk reduction?") and Q7 ("What if we stop measuring outcomes altogether?") are provocative in a way that could lead to harmful reductions in a program working with vulnerable minors. The persona's "subtract everything possible" mode does not calibrate well to contexts where safeguards exist for good reason.

### Career-Change Control

The career-change topic is a false-positive trap for the Lawyer persona but also reveals interesting things about the Simplifier. The Simplifier produces 12 questions at q08-12 and 12 at q10-15, maintaining its standard output volume.

**Quality assessment:** Moderate. The persona's best questions here challenge the binary framing of "nursing vs. law" (Q3: "What if you simply stopped being an ICU nurse but didn't become a lawyer?") and probe whether the stated motivation is genuine (Q2: "What's the 20% of nursing that delivers 80% of your satisfaction?"). These are useful questions.

However, the career-change topic also exposes the Simplifier's core limitation most clearly: many questions are not open-ended brainstorming prompts but **coaching exercises**. Q10 ("What's the one-sentence version of why this makes sense?") is a personal reflection exercise. Q11 ("What if you didn't do this for three years, but did it for one weekend?") is a practical action suggestion. Q12 at q10-15 ("What's the one thing you're actually called to do?") is a clarifying question that belongs in a coaching session, not a brainstorming synthesis.

The career-change topic does not trigger false-positive inclusion (the Simplifier has no special affinity for it), but it demonstrates that the Simplifier's output mode is fundamentally evaluative/advisory rather than generative. This holds across all topics but is most visible on a personal decision-making topic where the subtractive lens maps to "what can you cut from your decision process?" rather than "what new possibilities should you consider?"

### Topic Sensitivity Verdict

**Topic sensitivity is High.** The quality gap between the strong-trigger topic (mobile-app) and weak-trigger topics is substantial:

- **mobile-app (strong trigger):** 10-12 high-quality questions with specific, data-grounded simplification proposals. Filler onset at ~12-13.
- **school-consolidation (moderate fit):** 8-10 useful questions with some genuinely creative alternatives to the default approach. Filler onset at ~10.
- **food-truck, property-management, wearable-device (weak triggers):** 6-8 useful questions before formulaic repetition sets in. Questions are reasonable but rarely produce insights that other personas would miss.
- **youth-mentorship (weak trigger with safety concerns):** 5-7 useful questions, with some that actively misapply the subtractive lens to contexts where safeguards are necessary.
- **career-change (control):** 5-7 useful questions that shade into coaching/advising rather than brainstorming.

The gap between the strong-trigger topic and weak-trigger topics is approximately 3-5 questions of genuine value. On its best topic, the Simplifier produces a full set of 10-12 strong questions. On its weakest, only about half the output adds value that Tier 1 personas would not cover.

**Volume targets should vary by topic type:** On a strong-trigger topic (explicit de-scoping/simplification goal), 8-10 questions would be optimal. On a weak-trigger topic, 6-8 would be more appropriate. However, since the orchestrator is meant to select Tier 3 personas only when there is genuine topic affinity, the weak-trigger volume question is somewhat moot — if the topic does not trigger inclusion, the persona should not be included at all.

## Phase 2B Viability Assessment

This is the central question for the Simplifier. The selection guide flagged it as "better as Phase 3 answerer than Phase 2B core generator." After reviewing all 14 test runs across 7 topics, I assess that this judgment is largely correct, with a narrow exception for topics that explicitly center simplification as a goal.

### Does it produce genuinely open-ended questions or primarily convergent evaluations?

**Primarily convergent evaluations.** The vast majority of Simplifier questions follow the pattern: "What if we removed/eliminated/simplified X?" This is an evaluative frame — it asks whether X should exist, not what else might exist in X's place. Genuinely open-ended questions explore new territory; convergent evaluations narrow existing territory. The Simplifier narrows.

Quantitative estimate across all 14 test runs:
- **Convergent evaluation ("should we remove X?"):** ~60-65% of questions
- **Convergent-but-productive reframing ("what if the constraint is actually an advantage?"):** ~15-20% of questions
- **Genuinely open-ended ("what new approach emerges if we remove this assumption?"):** ~15-20% of questions

The 15-20% of genuinely open-ended questions are valuable, but they are embedded in a matrix of convergent evaluation that skews the overall synthesis toward subtraction.

### Does its subtractive framing complement or conflict with the synthesis group's output?

**Risks conflicting.** In Phase 2C synthesis, the Simplifier's questions would be cross-referenced with Tier 1 outputs from the Questioner (broad curiosity), Analyst (systematic evaluation), Visionary (expansive possibility), and others. The synthesis process reconciles these perspectives into a unified question set.

The problem: the Simplifier's questions systematically argue against exploring directions that other personas want to open. The Visionary asks "What if we expanded to...?" while the Simplifier asks "What if we never expanded?" The Questioner asks "What possibilities exist in the social feature space?" while the Simplifier asks "What if we cut social features entirely?" In synthesis, these opposing tensions are productive only if the synthesis agent can hold both perspectives simultaneously. In practice, having 8-10 "remove this" questions in the synthesis input risks creating a subtractive gravitational pull that narrows the final question set.

This effect would be amplified on weak-trigger topics where the Simplifier's removals are not well-grounded. On mobile-app, "What if we cut to one feature?" is genuinely useful because the topic demands simplification. On food-truck, "What if you never expanded?" is a reasonable question but one that could suppress productive exploration of growth strategies.

### Would its questions be better posed directly in Phase 3 rather than generated independently?

**Yes, in most cases.** The Simplifier's core questions — "Is this feature/component/plan element actually necessary?" — are evaluation questions that make more sense when applied to a specific proposal (Phase 3) than when generating open-ended exploration directions (Phase 2B). In Phase 3, a user already has brainstorming output and is developing an action plan. Asking "which of these ideas can we eliminate?" at that stage is directly productive. Asking "what if we eliminated things?" as a brainstorming question in Phase 2B is less useful because it runs counter to the brainstorming objective of expanding the possibility space.

The exception is when the user's stated goal is explicitly about simplification/de-scoping (like the mobile-app topic). In those cases, the Simplifier's Phase 2B questions are directly on-target because the brainstorming space IS the simplification space.

### Recommendation: Full Exclusion from Phase 2B

**Exclude from Phase 2B entirely. No conditional retention.**

The Simplifier's convergent, evaluative orientation conflicts with Phase 2B's generative purpose. Its high overlap with the Analyst (50-60%) means that the distinctive territory it covers (~25-30% of its output) is narrow, and much of that narrow territory consists of radical removal proposals better suited to Phase 3 evaluation.

**Conditional retention revoked.** The initial R6 assessment recommended narrow conditional retention for topics with explicit simplification/de-scoping goals (like mobile-app). A follow-up cross-persona viability analysis ([R6_simplifier-mobile-app-viability.md](R6_simplifier-mobile-app-viability.md)) tested this exception by comparing all Simplifier questions on the mobile-app topic against the output of every other persona. The result was decisive: **0 of 27 Simplifier questions covered territory unaddressed by other personas.** The simplification/de-scoping space is fully saturated by existing personas — particularly the Analyst, First Principles Thinker, Questioner, Constraint Flipper, and Devil's Advocate. The Constraint Flipper serves as a strictly superior "simplification persona" because it addresses the same territory with a generative orientation ("What if this constraint is an advantage?") rather than the Simplifier's convergent orientation ("What if we removed X?").

Since the Simplifier produces zero unique territory even on its strongest possible trigger topic (mobile-app was specifically designed to maximize its value), the orchestrator overhead of detecting trigger language and invoking the persona would produce no unique contribution to the final question set.

## Filler Analysis

### Filler Characterization

Simplifier filler falls into four categories, ordered by frequency:

1. **Recursive application of "remove X" to diminishing targets** (~40% of filler): After the major assumptions have been challenged, the persona applies its removal lens to progressively less significant elements. Food-truck's "What if you picked one day of the week to be closed?" and property-management's "What if the company eliminated all proactive owner communication except one monthly report?" are operational suggestions masquerading as brainstorming questions.

2. **The "one thing" convergence formula** (~25% of filler): "What's the one thing...?", "What's the one-sentence version...?", "What is the single essential...?" This formula appears at least once per topic and up to three times in 15-question sets. It is a legitimate Simplifier move on first use but becomes a recognizable crutch.

3. **Phase 3 coaching questions mislabeled as Phase 2B brainstorming** (~20% of filler): Questions that ask the user to narrow their thinking or make a decision rather than explore new territory. "What's the one-sentence version of why this makes sense?" and "What is the single essential action you want to take daily?" are reflection prompts, not brainstorming questions.

4. **Generic simplification moves not grounded in topic specifics** (~15% of filler): "What if we cut the timeline in half?", "What if we simply waited?", "Which competitor analysis doesn't matter?" These are generic business/project simplification heuristics that could be applied to any topic without domain knowledge.

## Cross-Topic Consistency

The Simplifier is highly consistent in its cognitive mode across all 7 topics. The following patterns hold regardless of topic:

1. **Cluster structure:** 3-4 clusters at q08-12, expanding to 4 at q10-15. Clusters are always organized around: (a) core value/scope reduction, (b) operational/structural simplification, (c) assumption challenging via removal, and optionally (d) meta-level simplification. This structure is invariant.

2. **Question framing:** "What if we removed/eliminated/simplified...?" accounts for 75-85% of questions across all topics. The subtractive frame is the persona's identity and does not vary by domain.

3. **Cluster count stability:** Unlike some personas that expand clusters at higher volumes, the Simplifier maintains a tight 3-4 cluster structure even at 15 questions. This means individual clusters get larger (4-5 questions each) rather than new thematic territories being opened. This is consistent with the persona's convergent nature — it deepens its existing lines of inquiry rather than opening new ones.

4. **Quality-volume curve shape:** Consistent across all topics. Strong start (Q1-8), noticeable degradation (Q9-12), marked filler (Q13+). The curve shape does not change; only the absolute quality level shifts based on topic affinity.

5. **Absence of open-ended divergence:** In no topic does the Simplifier produce a genuinely divergent cluster — one that opens unexpected territory. Even its best questions (food-truck Q1 on three-item menu, mobile-app Q2 on user willingness-to-pay-to-keep) are convergent reframings, not divergent explorations. This consistency confirms that the persona's cognitive mode is fundamentally evaluative.

## Summary Recommendation

**Fully exclude from Phase 2B. No conditional retention.** The Simplifier's convergent, evaluative orientation makes it a poor fit for the generative purpose of Phase 2B question generation. Its 50-60% overlap with the Analyst means most of its useful territory is already covered by Tier 1, and its distinctive contribution (radical removal proposals) risks creating subtractive bias in synthesis. Cross-persona viability analysis on the mobile-app topic (strongest possible trigger) confirmed that 0 of 27 Simplifier questions cover territory unaddressed by other personas — the simplification space is fully saturated by existing personas, with the Constraint Flipper as a strictly superior alternative.

**Redirect the Simplifier's strengths to Phase 3.** The persona's core cognitive mode — evaluating what should be removed — is well-suited to Phase 3 action planning, where proposals exist and need to be assessed for necessity and scope. A Phase 3 "simplification pass" that applies the Simplifier's lens to a draft action plan would likely be more valuable than Phase 2B question generation.
